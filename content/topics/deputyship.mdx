## What deputyship is

Deputyship is a court process. It allows the court to appoint a person, called
a deputy, to make certain decisions on behalf of an adult who is not able to
make those decisions themselves. The deputy might be given the power to make
decisions about money, or about care and welfare, or both, depending on what
the court decides.

Unlike an LPA, which a person sets up themselves while they are still able to
decide, deputyship is applied for when a person is already unable to make the
decisions in question.

## When it tends to matter

Deputyship often comes up when:

- An adult is not able to make certain decisions for themselves.
- No LPA was put in place while the person was able to make one.
- Someone needs the legal authority to act for the person, for example to
  manage money or arrange care.

## Thinking about the person being cared for

Deputyship is meant to support a person who cannot make a particular decision,
not to take away more control than is needed. Good practice is to involve the
person as much as possible in decisions that affect them, and to make decisions
in their best interests. Many adults can make some decisions and not others,
and that is normal. The aim is to support choice wherever it exists.

If the person is able to understand and make the choice themselves, an LPA may
be worth looking at instead. See the LPA topic.
